How can you create a fictional planet or solar system and keep it believable? Especially in hard science fiction, making a mistake that makes the planet simply impossible in real life might annoy an audience if they can see why it is impossible. And as an author, you may just find it satisfying to know that the planets you create are genuinely possible.

The question is really about avoiding mistakes. And the most likely mistakes are probably in the star system's thermodynamics and chemistry or in its orbit. For both these, we have a history and a present, and they can both be wrong.

To start with, here's a brief overview of planet formation.

Planet Formation: Dynamics

This is about the mass and orbit of the planets only.

It is thought that most solar systems form in roughly the same way: Galaxies have a lot of gas and dust floating around in them. In more dense regions of gas and dust, a collapse can occur where the densest regions become so heavy that they gravitationally attract more and more gas and dust until the whole mess forms a huge ball of gas --- a new star. Because the original gas cloud probably had some random angular momentum, the result is a star in the centre, and a lot of gas and dust in orbit around it. Any pocket of gas/dust that is orbiting in a different plane or the opposite direction will eventually crash into other gas/dust and the result is that a disk forms with most of the gas circling in the same plane and direction.

IMAGE OF Protoplanetary nebula.

This is the normal starting point for discussions of planet formation: A central star, surrounded by a disk of gas and dust. The next step is for the gas and dust to form planets. This process is not fully understood: There is debate about which mechanisms are usually dominant, and there are problems that are not fully resolved.

The gas and dust in the protoplanetary disk, in order to form planets, has to stick together. The simplest model for this (which is at least slightly wrong) is that dust sticks together to form pebbles, and these gradually stick together with other pebbles all the way until we have planets.

The two problems with this is that metre-sized rocks would spiral inwards too quickly in a gas cloud (because the gas cloud actually moves slightly more slowly than the orbital speed of the rock), and it takes such a long time to form planets that the hydrogen gas dissipates before it can form large gas giants.

It is thought that instabilities and fluctuations within protoplanetary disks can bypass both of these problems, and so the conclusion reached is largely thought to be correct: The early solar system produced very many planetesimals that then interact gravitationally and through collisions to create the final solar system.

The system can be approximated by several thousand planetesimals, and an N-body simulation keeps track of how they interact over the next million years or so. An example of this type of simulation dates back to 2002: Formation of Protoplanet Systems and Diversity of Planetary Systems.

The results, if done properly, are a number of planets, spaced out reasonably similarly to in our solar system, and all on relatively normal orbits. The interaction between many small planetesimals and larger planets tends to push the heavier planets into "nice" orbits.

However, close encounters between planets can push them onto eccentric orbits, and in fact it seems that our solar system has unusually circular orbits. A paper from 2014 discusses exoplanet eccentricity: The Exoplanet Orbital Eccentricity - Multiplicity Eccentricity - Multiplicity Relation and the Solar System, and refers to several reviews that discuss this phenomenon in more detail, before discussing the observation that systems with many planets tend to have more circular orbits.

Another important consideration is that beyond a certain point (which appears to be at about 2.7 astronomical units for our solar system according to wikipedia), water ice was stable in the protoplanetary disk, and this created very many more planetesimals beyond that point --- which encourages or is necessary for the formation of giant planets (presumably because having more material from which to grow planets leads to faster planet growth, and they can reach a size large enough to gather hydrogen gas sooner).

So where does this leave us in terms of making sure that planets are realistic? Really there are two answers depending on how much effort the reader is willing to go to: The high-effort answer is that you can realistically simulate the solar system from the formation of thousands of planetesimals to the presence of between 1 and several handfuls of planets, and the resulting planets would have a realistic history. This kind of simulation will take a long time to run, though, between days and months --- the dynamics need to be resolved down to timescales shorter than an hour, but the simulation has to run to perhaps half a million years. The low-effort answer is that the planet-formation phase doesn't really impose much of a constraint on what's possible for a single planet. Planets can move around. They can be on circular orbits or they can be on eccentric orbits (more likely if there are fewer planets in your solar system). There is no fundamental reason why large planets can't be close in, far out, etc.

However, there is always a big constraint that any multi-planet system should be dynamically stable on timescales of millions of years. This is because a solar system that is fluctuating on fast timescales would eventually remove planets until it stabilised again ... the solar system effectively searches out stable configurations and stays in those for long periods of time. So if an intrepid explorer stumbles upon a 2 billion year old solar system, it is unlikely that that system would be undergoing 1000-year changes, unless the explorer had specifically sought out a fluctuating system.

Summary

  • Our understanding of solar system formation places few constraints on the mass or orbit of planets around realistic solar systems. It seems that it is possible for planets of any size to be anywhere.
  • It is important, though, that a realistic solar system is dynamically stable on timescales of millions of years.

Planet Formation: Chemical composition

Our solar system tends to have rocky planets near the sun and icy planets further out. This is something that is somewhat important for a solar system to be believable. The story actually starts in the first stages of planet formation when grains of dust come together to form pebbles: Ice sublimes when it gets to too warm, and so the pebbles that form in the inner solar system are largely rocky. (see wikipedia's frost line). The same goes for CO2 ice. So planetesimals that start closer in tend to be depleted in elements that prefer to be in chemicals with low boiling points.
